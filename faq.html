Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about your rights and resources.

  • Yes. The U.S. Constitution protects everyone within U.S. borders, regardless of citizenship or immigration status. This includes:

    • The right to remain silent (Fifth Amendment)
    • Protection from unreasonable searches (Fourth Amendment)
    • The right to an attorney in criminal proceedings
    • Due process of law

    Learn more on our Know Your Rights page.

  • No. You do not have to open your door. You can ask through a closed door:

    • "Who are you?"
    • "Do you have a warrant signed by a judge?"

    An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) does not give them the right to enter your home. Only a judicial warrant signed by a judge allows entry without your consent.

  • Stay calm and follow these steps:

    1. Ask: "Am I free to leave?" If yes, walk away calmly.
    2. You have the right to remain silent. Say: "I am exercising my right to remain silent."
    3. You do not have to answer questions about your immigration status.
    4. If detained, say: "I want to speak to a lawyer."
    5. Do not run, argue, or resist physically.

  • Yes. The Fourth Amendment protects you from unreasonable searches. You can say:

    "I do not consent to a search."

    However, if an officer has a warrant or "probable cause," they may search anyway. Refusing consent does not give them probable cause. Even if they search, your refusal is on record and may help in court.

  • Many organizations provide free or low-cost legal services:

    • RAICES: 1-800-354-9365
    • National Immigrant Justice Center: (212) 629-6335
    • ACLU Immigrants' Rights Project: (212) 549-1200

    Visit our Local Resources page for more organizations.

  • It depends on your state. Some states have "stop and identify" laws that require you to give your name if police have reasonable suspicion of a crime.

    However, you are generally not required to:

    • Show a driver's license (unless driving)
    • Provide your immigration status
    • Answer questions beyond your name (in stop-and-identify states)

    Check your state's specific laws for details.

  • No. Only U.S. citizens can vote in federal, state, and most local elections. Voting as a non-citizen is a crime and can affect your immigration status.

    However, there are other ways to participate in civic life:

    • Attend town halls and community meetings
    • Contact elected officials about issues you care about
    • Support voter registration drives
    • Volunteer with community organizations

  • A "sanctuary city" (or county, or state) is a place that limits cooperation with federal immigration enforcement. Policies vary, but generally:

    • Local police don't ask about immigration status
    • Local jails don't hold people solely for ICE
    • City resources aren't used for immigration enforcement

    Being in a sanctuary city doesn't make you immune from federal immigration enforcement, but it may provide some additional protection.

  • Create a family preparedness plan:

    • Designate a trusted contact who can care for children or handle finances
    • Keep important documents in a safe, accessible place (birth certificates, passports, medical records)
    • Memorize key phone numbers for family, lawyers, and community organizations
    • Create a power of attorney to designate someone to act on your behalf
    • Talk to your children about what to do if you're not home
